The methodology centers on identifying the moment price overextends its normal range, creating a spring that inevitably snaps back toward the target line. ## The Five-Wave Structure of the Wolfe Pattern

To successfully implement the strategy, you must clearly identify five key points. In a bullish model, points 1, 3, and 5 are local lows, while 2 and 4 are highs. The golden rule: point 3 must be lower than point 1, and point 5 must be lower than point 3, creating the visual effect of a tapering descending wedge. Additionally, point 2 must be a clear high between 1 and 3, and point 4 must be higher than point 1 but lower than point 2. Special attention is paid to the Sweet Zone — the area below the line drawn through points 1 and 3, where point 5 forms. This is the zone where a trader hunts for reversal signals. The geometry of the pattern dictates the logic: the market is oversold, the momentum of the move is drying up, and potential is building for a massive impulse toward the Estimated Price at Arrival (EPA) line, which is drawn through points 1 and 4.

## Synthesizing Fibonacci Levels and Waves

Integrating Fibonacci levels turns a visual pattern into a precise trading system. In an ideal Wolfe model, wave 2-3 is an extension of 127% to 161.8% of wave 1-2. Wave 4 most often corrects to the 61.8% or 78.6% level of wave 2-3. The most critical moment is finding point 5. Traders use a Fibonacci extension applied to the 3-4 segment for this purpose. Point 5 often aligns with the 1.272 or 1.618 levels of this extension. If the Fibonacci projection coincides with the graphic 1-3-5 line, the probability of a bounce increases significantly. Such confluence of different technical analysis methods is known as convergence. Using the grid helps filter out false signals where the price has not yet reached the exhaustion zone or, conversely, has breached it too aggressively, invalidating the reversal scenario.

## Entry Algorithm

Entry into a position does not happen the moment the price touches the 1-3 line, but rather upon the appearance of confirming signals within the Sweet Zone. Experienced analysts recommend waiting for the price to reclaim the 1-3-5 line or for a reversal candlestick pattern (such as a pin bar or engulfing candle) to form directly at the 161.8% Fibonacci level. The stop-loss is placed behind the nearest local extreme at point 5, with a small buffer for volatility. Using Fibonacci levels makes the stop-loss more data-driven: if the price drops below the 200% extension of 3-4, the pattern is considered broken, and the risk of further downside increases. Thus, a mathematical approach minimizes the emotional factor, allowing the trader to act strictly according to the protocol during periods of high market turbulence.

## Defining Targets and Taking Profit

The primary objective of the strategy is the EPA line (1-4). However, the price often encounters resistance on the way to this target, which can be forecasted using retracement levels. The first intermediate target (Take Profit 1) is usually the level of point 4.
