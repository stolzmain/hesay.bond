## Identifying Imbalance zones

To identify imbalance zones, you must analyze candlestick charts. A classic FVG is formed when there is no overlap between the wicks of the first and third candles. Specifically, the lower wick of the first candle does not overlap with the upper wick of the third candle (for a bearish FVG), or the upper wick of the first candle does not overlap with the lower wick of the third candle (for a bullish FVG). The middle candle in this formation often features a significant body, indicating strong momentum. These zones serve as an indicator that the market has not reached "fair value" in that range and has a high probability of returning for a correction.

## The nature of FVG formation

The primary reason for FVG formation is that major players (institutional traders, market makers) may enter the market with large volumes that cannot be instantly absorbed. This creates an imbalance that the market subsequently seeks to compensate for. Such zones often remain unfilled after a strong move, signaling liquidity that can be tapped into when the price returns. Understanding this dynamic allows traders to forecast potential reversal or correction points.

## FVG trading strategies

Trading from imbalance zones is based on the assumption that the market tends to fill these inefficient price ranges.

Entry on retest: Often, after an FVG forms, the price returns to this zone to fill it. Traders can open positions when the price enters the FVG zone or reaches its midpoint (the 50% level), expecting a reversal in the direction of the initial impulse.

Stop-loss placement: The stop-loss is typically placed outside the FVG zone to protect the position if the market continues to move against the expected reversal.

Take-profit targets: Targets can be set based on previous highs/lows, Fibonacci levels, or other key liquidity zones.

## Risk management and confirmations

Effective FVG trading requires strict risk management. Not every imbalance zone leads to a perfect retest and reversal. It is important to use additional confirmations:

Timeframes: Analyzing FVG across multiple timeframes can significantly improve signal quality. FVGs on higher timeframes (4H, Daily) carry more weight.

Volume: An increase in volume as the price approaches an FVG zone may signal its significance.

Order Blocks: Combining FVGs with Order Blocks—areas where large players place their orders—can provide stronger trading signals.

Indices and correlations: For cryptocurrencies, tracking Bitcoin's behavior and its correlation with other assets can provide additional context.

Overall, FVGs represent a powerful tool for understanding internal market structure and forecasting future price movements. These are not merely gaps on a chart, but evidence of inefficiencies that the market seeks to correct. Incorporating imbalance analysis into your trading strategy can significantly increase the accuracy of your entries and exits, improving your overall performance in the volatile cryptocurrency market.
