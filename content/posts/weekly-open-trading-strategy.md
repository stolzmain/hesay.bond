For institutional market participants, the WO serves as a reference point, the fair value of the current five-day period relative to which market sentiment is formed. Unlike lagging indicators, this level is static and objective. It divides the market space into two zones: the buyers' dominance zone (above WO) and the sellers' dominance zone (below WO). Understanding how the price interacts with this level allows a trader not just to follow the chart, but to anticipate the intentions of big players, who often use the weekly open to build positions or manipulate liquidity.

## Determining the market movement vector

Using the Weekly Open begins with identifying the mid-term bias. If the asset's current price is above the weekly open level, priority is given to long positions. If it is below, the focus is on shorts. This simple rule allows for filtering out counter-trend signals and trading in harmony with the weekly cycle. However, professional analysis requires a deeper approach. The WO level is rarely pierced by price without a reaction; it more often acts as a magnet or dynamic support/resistance. When the price moves away from the opening level too quickly without a prior test, the probability of a return to it for rebalancing during the week increases significantly. For an analyst, this is a signal of potential overbought or oversold conditions within the weekly range.

## The Power of 3 concept

The strategy of trading from the WO is inextricably linked to the Power of 3 (PO3) algorithm, which includes accumulation, manipulation, and distribution. In the context of a weekly chart, this looks as follows: at the beginning of the week (Monday to Tuesday morning), the price consolidates near the opening level. This is the accumulation phase. Then, a false move against the true direction of the week often occurs—the manipulation. If we are expecting a bullish week, the price must first go below the WO level to sweep liquidity (buy-side stop losses) and allow institutions to enter longs at more favorable prices. After the weak hands are shaken out, the true distribution begins—a powerful impulse move that carries the price far beyond the opening level toward targets at the highs of previous periods.

## Judas Swing at the start of the week

Traders pay special attention to the so-called Judas Swing. This is a classic trap occurring on Monday or Tuesday. The market mimics a breakout in one direction relative to the Weekly Open, provoking retail players to open positions in the direction of the move. In reality, this movement is intended solely to capture liquidity. For a professional, the price's return to the opening level after such a false breakout is a key signal to act. If, after a deep dive below the WO, the price sharply returns and consolidates above the level, this confirms the buyers' strength. It is at this point, upon a retest of the opening level from above, that the most mathematically advantageous entry point is formed, with a tight stop loss and high profit potential.

## Confirmation patterns and entry

Trading from the Weekly Open should not be a matter of blindly placing limit orders. Effective entry requires confirmation on lower timeframes (M15 or H1). A professional algorithm involves looking for a Market Structure Shift after interacting with the WO level. When the price touches the Weekly Open level, we look for the formation of a reversal pattern or engulfment.
