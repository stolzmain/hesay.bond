The primary source of this liquidity is the protective orders of retail traders: stop-losses. The Stop Hunt concept describes the process where price is intentionally directed toward these order clusters to trigger them. When a buy stop-loss (on short positions) is triggered, it turns into a market buy order, which a large player uses as counterparty liquidity to fill their long position or open a short. Understanding the location of these liquidity pools gives traders a massive edge, allowing them to avoid falling victim to manipulation and instead use it as a signal to enter a trade.

## Order Heatmap as a Visual Filter

Traditional charts do not provide insight into what is happening inside the order book, but a heatmap changes the game. A heatmap is a visual representation of the order book in a historical perspective. It displays order density using various colors: bright zones (yellow, orange) signal large limit barriers, while dark zones indicate a lack of interest. From a Stop Hunt perspective, a heatmap allows one to see magnets for price. Often, large players place significant limit orders just above or below key support and resistance levels, enticing the price to move there. By observing the heatmap, a professional analyst sees not just a graphic pattern, but an actual confrontation of volumes. When price approaches a bright cluster on the heatmap and a sharp absorption of liquidity occurs followed by a reversal, it confirms that a hunt has been completed.

## Mechanics of Provocation and Stop Raids

The Stop Hunt process usually involves several stages. First, an obvious level is formed (e.g., Equal Lows), which retail traders perceive as reliable support. Stop orders begin to accumulate behind this level. Large capital, possessing sufficient volume, initiates an impulse move downward, breaking this level. At this moment, two events occur in the market: the stop-losses of those who were long are triggered, and the Sell Stop orders of breakout traders are activated. Both of these streams create a huge volume of market sell orders. The institutional player absorbs these sales with their limit buy orders, filling their position at optimal prices. On the heatmap, this looks like a touch of a dense price zone followed by an immediate bounce. If, after breaking the level, the price quickly returns back, we are dealing with a false breakout or a Swing Failure Pattern (SFP), which is a classic sign of a liquidity raid.

## Entry Strategy After Liquidity Sweeps

Working with the Stop Hunt concept requires patience. Instead of trying to guess whether a level will hold, a professional trader waits for it to be swept. The main entry signal appears when the liquidity has already been taken. On the chart, this is often accompanied by the formation of a long wick (pin bar) or a sharp engulfing candle. Using a heatmap at this moment is critical: if we see that after the stop-loss sweep, the high density of limit orders on the heatmap has disappeared or moved to the opposite side, it confirms that the large player has satisfied their demand. The entry is executed upon the price returning to the previous range or during a retest of the manipulation zone (Order Block).
