The core concept is that in an uptrend, the closing price generally settles above the opening price, while in a downtrend, it settles below. This indicator helps traders assess the direction and strength of market volatility and predict potential trend reversals.

## Calculation principle and components

The RVI is calculated by comparing the difference between the closing and opening prices to the total price range (High - Low) over a specific period, typically 10 or 14. While various formulas exist, the general logic boils down to evaluating how close the closing price is to the top or bottom of the trading range. These values are then smoothed using moving averages. The indicator is displayed in a separate window below the price chart and consists of two lines: the main RVI line (often green) and a signal line (usually red), which is a smoothed moving average of the main RVI line, most commonly a 4-period average. RVI values fluctuate in a range from 0 to 100, with 50 acting as the centerline.

## Interpretation of RVI values

Similar to other oscillators, the RVI allows for the identification of overbought and oversold market states, as well as confirming ongoing trends. High RVI values, typically above 50, indicate increasing volatility toward the upside, suggesting bullish momentum. Conversely, values below 50 signify increasing volatility toward the downside, indicating bearish momentum.

Overbought and oversold levels are often identified at the 80 and 20 marks, respectively. An RVI breach above 80 may signal overbought conditions, while a drop below 20 may suggest oversold conditions. However, it is vital to remember that the indicator can remain in extreme zones for extended periods during strong trends and does not always herald an immediate reversal.

## Trading strategies with RVI

The RVI offers several effective strategies for traders:

 Line crossovers. The most common strategy is based on the crossover of the main RVI line and the signal line. A bullish signal occurs when the RVI crosses above the signal line, indicating an increase in bullish momentum. A bearish signal is formed when the RVI crosses the signal line from top to bottom, signaling growing bearish pressure. The reliability of these signals increases when crossovers occur away from the centerline.
 Divergences. This is a powerful signal that occurs when the price action of an asset and the RVI diverge. Bullish divergence is observed when the price forms lower lows while the RVI forms higher lows, predicting a potential upward reversal. Bearish divergence manifests when the price hits higher highs, but the RVI forms lower highs, which can indicate weakening bullish momentum and a potential decline. Divergences often serve as early indicators of potential trend changes.
 Trend confirmation. RVI can be used to confirm the strength of an existing trend. An ascending RVI in a bull market confirms its strength, while a descending RVI in a bear market confirms its continuation. This is particularly useful for trend following.
 Overbought/oversold states. In sideways or consolidating markets, the RVI exiting overbought (above 80) or oversold (below 20) zones can serve as an entry signal.
