## The Percentage Price Oscillator (PPO)
The Percentage Price Oscillator (PPO) is a technical momentum indicator that displays the relationship between two moving averages, expressed as a percentage. It is similar to MACD, but instead of absolute values, it uses percentage changes, making it useful for comparing momentum across different assets or analyzing an asset over time without being tied to its absolute price. PPO is calculated as the difference between a shorter and longer exponential moving average (EMA), divided by the longer EMA, and multiplied by 100. The result is a histogram and a signal line, which is a moving average of the PPO itself.

## Applying PPO to Range Trading
PPO can be a powerful tool for range traders, helping to identify overbought or oversold conditions within established boundaries. When PPO reaches extreme positive values (above the signal line and peaking), it may indicate that the asset is overbought and nearing a resistance level. Conversely, when PPO reaches extreme negative values (below the signal line and bottoming out), it can signal that the asset is oversold and near a support level. Traders can use PPO crossovers with its signal line to confirm potential entry and exit points: an upward crossover may signal a buy, while a downward crossover indicates a sell.

## Entry and Exit Strategies
To enter a long position in a range, traders look for confirmation that the price is approaching the support level. PPO can serve as an additional filter: when the price is near support and the PPO begins to rise or crosses its signal line from below, it can be a strong buy signal. For sell orders, the situation is reversed: the price approaches resistance, and the PPO declines or crosses the signal line from above, indicating a potential spot to sell. Stop-losses are typically placed slightly below the support level for long positions and slightly above the resistance level for short positions. Take-profit is set at the opposite boundary of the range.

## Risk Management
Risk management is critical in range trading, as a breakout from the range can lead to significant losses. It is essential to determine the appropriate position size based on the distance to the stop-loss and total capital. Never risk more than 1-2 percent of your trading account on a single trade. Using trailing stop-losses can also help protect profits after a trade moves in a favorable direction but remains within the range. Additionally, traders must be prepared to close the position quickly if the range is broken, as the momentum following a breakout can be very strong.
