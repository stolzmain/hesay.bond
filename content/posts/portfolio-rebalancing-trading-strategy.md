For example, during periods of rapid stock market growth, the share of stocks may increase from a target 60% to 75%, which automatically raises the portfolio's risk profile. Rebalancing involves selling a portion of assets that have appreciated and buying those that have lagged or decreased in value. Thus, the investor locks in profits in overheated sectors and reallocates capital into undervalued instruments, which is the fundamental embodiment of the buy low, sell high principle.

## Calendar and Threshold Methods

There are two main approaches to implementing this strategy. The first is calendar rebalancing, which involves reviewing the portfolio at fixed intervals: quarterly, semi-annually, or annually. This method is convenient due to its predictability and minimal time spent on monitoring. However, it ignores sharp market movements that occur within the chosen interval. The second approach is threshold rebalancing. Here, the trigger is a deviation of an asset share from the target value by a certain percentage (e.g., 5%). This method is more adaptive and allows for more effective utilization of market volatility by capturing abnormal movements as soon as they occur. A combined approach, which blends scheduled portfolio reviews with threshold triggers, is considered the most professional and balanced for large institutional portfolios.

## Profiting from Market Noise

One of the key advantages of regular rebalancing is the ability to monetize volatility, turning market noise into additional yield. In a sideways trend or during cyclical fluctuations, assets constantly trade places: one rises while another falls. By systematically cycling capital from winners to losers, the trader averages the entry cost for promising positions and prevents the portfolio from becoming overly concentrated. This creates a mathematical expectancy of profit, often referred to as the rebalancing bonus. In the long term, this approach yields a higher return per unit of risk (Sharpe ratio) compared to a buy and hold strategy, especially during periods of prolonged global market instability.

## The Mathematical Advantage of Shannon's Demon

Professional analysts often associate the success of rebalancing with the concept of Shannon's Demon. This is a mathematical model proving that even if two assets have a zero expected arithmetic mean return but exhibit high volatility and low correlation, regular rebalancing between them will lead to exponential growth of the total capital. This occurs due to the constant extraction of excess value from a volatile asset and preserving it in a less volatile instrument until the next cycle. For a trader, this means that selecting instruments with low correlation (e.g., gold and tech stocks) within a rebalanced portfolio is more important than attempting to predict the price direction of any specific asset.

## Transaction Cost Optimization

When implementing this strategy, it is critical to consider the impact of fees and taxes. Frequent rebalancing trades can significantly erode net returns.
