The logic of the indicator is simple: if the price closes in the upper part of its range on increasing volume, accumulation (buying) prevails in the market. CMF allows for the identification of buying or selling pressure, confirming the sustainability of the current trend or signaling its imminent end.

## Technical Parameters and Indicator Setup

To work correctly with this strategy, the standard indicator period is set to 21. This value is considered optimal as it covers approximately one trading month, smoothing out short-term noise while maintaining sensitivity to medium-term trend changes. Visually, the CMF is a line that fluctuates above and below the zero mark in a range from -1 to +1. A trader's primary focus should be on the position of the line relative to the horizontal 0 level. Positive values indicate bull strength, while negative values indicate bear dominance. It is important to understand that CMF is not just a momentum indicator, but a reality check for price movement. If the price is rising but CMF is falling or stays below zero, such growth is considered unsustainable and is likely a fakeout or a short-term correction.

## Zero-Line Crossover Strategy

The most common and effective way to use CMF is trading on the zero-line crossover. When the indicator crosses the 0 level from bottom to top and holds in the positive zone, it generates a buy signal. This means that capital inflow into the asset exceeds its outflow. However, professionals recommend using a filter: a signal is considered valid if the CMF line holds above zero for several bars and reaches a value of +0.10. Similarly, for short positions: crossing the zero line from top to bottom while reaching the -0.10 mark confirms the beginning of a distribution phase. The advantage of this method is that it allows for entry at the inception of a new trend, when institutional players begin to accumulate positions, which inevitably leads to a subsequent strong price impulse.

## Divergence as a Leading Signal

Divergence between the price chart and the CMF line is one of the most reliable trend reversal signals in technical analysis. Bullish divergence occurs when an asset's price hits a lower low, while the CMF indicator forms a higher low. This is a clear sign that selling pressure is drying up and smart money is starting to scoop up the asset despite the ongoing price decline. Bearish divergence is observed when the price reaches a new peak, but the CMF high is lower than the previous one. This signals capital outflow against a backdrop of rising prices—a classic trap for retail traders. Using divergences allows a trader to exit a position before the main dump begins or enter a trade at the absolute pivot point, ensuring a superior risk-to-reward ratio.

## Signal Filtering with Price Levels

Despite its effectiveness, CMF should not be used in total isolation. The strategy shows the best results when combining money flow data with classic support and resistance levels.
