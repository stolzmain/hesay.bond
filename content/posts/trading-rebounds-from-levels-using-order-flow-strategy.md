## Identifying Liquidity Concentration Zones

Effective execution of this strategy starts with proper chart mark-up. Instead of subjective trendlines, a professional analyst uses Volume Profile. Key benchmarks include POC (Point of Control) levels from previous sessions, Value Area boundaries (VAH/VAL), and High Volume Nodes. It is at these points that maximum liquidity is concentrated, and where large players are most likely to defend their positions. When the price approaches such a level, Order Flow helps determine whether this approach is a movement climax or the start of a true breakout. It is important to understand that a level is not a thin line, but a zone where we expect to see specific activity in the Time and Sales window and footprint.

## Market Mechanics Analysis Tools

A standard candlestick chart is insufficient to implement this strategy. A trader needs a footprint (cluster chart) and cumulative delta. The footprint visualizes executed market orders within each candle, allowing you to see the imbalance between buys and sells at specific price levels. Cumulative delta, in turn, shows the overall market pressure over a period. If the price approaches resistance on a rising positive delta, but stops moving higher, we get the first signal of iceberg orders or massive limit supply. Using the Order Book (DOM) also provides an edge, allowing you to track the placement of large limit densities that act as physical barriers for the price.

## Signs of Exhaustion and Absorption

The mechanics of a level rejection are built on two market processes: exhaustion and absorption. Exhaustion is characterized by a sharp decrease in volume and market participant activity as the price approaches a level—the attacking side simply runs out of ammunition. Absorption looks different: market orders continue to pour in (aggressive delta), but the price cannot move even a tick because all market orders are being bought or sold into the limit orders of a large player. In the footprint, this is reflected as large clusters skewed toward the breakout, located right at the level boundary, followed by an impulse in the opposite direction. This is the ideal moment to enter a rejection trade.

## Entry Point Based on Footprint Signals

The ideal rejection setup forms when the price tests a level and we see a Failed Auction combined with visual confirmation in the clusters. A key pattern is the emergence of imbalances at the candle's extreme. For example, when testing resistance, large sell orders should appear in the upper wick of the candle, effectively pushing the price down. An additional filter is a negative delta on a rising candle, which directly indicates hidden selling.
