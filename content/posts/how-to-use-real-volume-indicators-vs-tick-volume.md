In the world of trading, volume analysis is a fundamental tool for understanding market dynamics and confirming price movements. However, not all volume metrics are created equal. There is a fundamental difference between tick volume and the real volume indicator, which can significantly impact the accuracy of your trading decisions.

## Tick Volume: Fundamentals and Limitations

Tick volume represents the number of price changes (ticks) within a specific timeframe. This metric is widely used, especially in decentralized markets like Forex and certain cryptocurrencies, where real trading volume data is not always available or centrally reported. Tick volume can serve as a proxy for market activity and participation intensity, helping traders gauge market sentiment, identify reversals, breakouts, and key support/resistance levels, particularly in intraday trading.

However, tick volume has significant limitations. It does not reflect the actual number of shares or contracts traded. A trade of one lot is counted the same as a trade of one hundred lots if both cause a price change. This blindness to size can distort the picture of real market activity. Furthermore, tick volume data can vary significantly across different brokers, undermining its consistency and reliability. In low-liquidity conditions, tick volume can also exaggerate real activity.

## Real Volume: Market Depth

Real volume, by contrast, represents the total number of shares, contracts, or units that actually changed hands during a specific period. This data is available in centralized markets, such as stock and futures exchanges. Real volume provides a precise view of money flows and market conviction, as it measures the actual quantity of assets moving between participants.

This metric is considered significantly more accurate and reliable, as it reflects genuine participation and activity. Real volume allows for a deeper understanding of market dynamics and is an essential part of supply and demand analysis.

## Differences and Advantages of Real Volume

The key distinction is that tick volume is merely a proxy for activity, whereas real volume is the exact quantity of assets traded. Where real volume is available, it is almost always preferred for deep and accurate analysis.

Using real volume gives a trader several advantages:

 Trend Confirmation: Rising prices on increasing real volume confirm the strength of an uptrend; falling prices on increasing real volume confirm the strength of a downtrend.
 Breakout Identification: Significant spikes in real volume accompanying a breakout confirm its validity and strength, whereas a breakout on low volume may be a fakeout.
 Reversal Detection: Divergence between price and volume (e.g., rising prices on falling volume) can signal trend exhaustion and a potential reversal. This indicates declining conviction from buyers or sellers.
 Tracking Smart Money: Real volume analysis provides better insight into the actions of large players who can significantly influence price.
 Forecasting: Real volume can act as a leading indicator, foreshadowing price changes.

## Application Strategies

To effectively use real volume, it must be integrated into a comprehensive trading strategy. When price breaks through a key support or resistance level, it is essential to look for confirmation in the form of a significant volume increase.
