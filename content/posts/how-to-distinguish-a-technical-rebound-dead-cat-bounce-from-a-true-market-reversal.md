##  Dead cat bounce: the essence
A dead cat bounce is a temporary, often sharp recovery in prices following a significant decline. The term stems from the adage that even a dead cat will bounce if dropped from a great height. Such bounces usually lack fundamental reasons for growth and are the result of purely technical factors. These include short covering by speculators, profit-taking on shorts, or automatic buys triggered by stop-loss orders. Trading volume during such a bounce is typically low or declining, which indicates a lack of strong interest from large buyers. After such a brief spike, the price often resumes its decline, breaking through previous lows.

##  Genuine reversal: key signs
A genuine market reversal, unlike a bounce, represents a more sustainable and fundamentally supported change in trend. It is rarely V-shaped and is usually formed gradually, creating bases such as double bottoms, inverted head and shoulders, or rounded bottoms. A key indicator of a true reversal is the emergence of real buying interest, often backed by improving macroeconomic data, corporate news, or shifts in sector prospects. The price begins to demonstrate higher lows and higher highs, gradually overcoming key resistance levels.

##  Volume and indicators
Volume analysis is one of the most reliable tools for distinguishing a bounce from a reversal. During a dead cat bounce, volume on the upside is usually low, while volume on the subsequent decline is high. This points to weakness in buyers and strength in sellers. In a true reversal, the situation is the opposite: trading volume increases as the price rises and decreases during corrections. This signals an influx of fresh capital and sustainable buying pressure.

Additionally, one should use oscillators and trend indicators. The Relative Strength Index (RSI), Stochastic, or MACD may show bullish divergences, where the price sets new lows while the indicators begin to rise, signaling a weakening of downward momentum. Breaking and holding above key moving averages (e.g., the 50-day or 200-day) are also strong reversal signals, especially if accompanied by an increase in volume.

##  Timeframes and context
It is important to view price action in the context of multiple timeframes. What looks like a bounce on the daily chart might be part of a larger reversal pattern on the weekly or monthly chart. Conversely, a local bounce on the hourly chart may be a minor correction within a long-term downtrend. The macroeconomic situation, overall market sentiment (bullish or bearish), and news related to a specific sector or company also play a significant role. The absence of substantial positive catalysts for price growth should warrant increased caution.

##  Strategies for traders
For effective trading under these conditions, it is necessary to employ a comprehensive approach. First, always wait for confirmation. Entering a position solely on the basis of a sharp rise after a decline is risky.
